# Agentropolis Economic Ontology

The Agentropolis Economic Ontology models how value is created, coordinated, exchanged, governed, and audited inside an AI-native city system.

It borrows useful language from classical and neoclassical growth theory, but does not assume perfect rationality, perfect information, or frictionless markets. Agentropolis treats coordination, governance, trust, data quality, compute access, and agent behavior as first-class economic objects.

## Core thesis

Classical and neoclassical economics often frame growth around labor, capital, savings, markets, equilibrium, and technology.

Agentropolis extends that frame:

AI-native growth comes from agents, ontology, coordination, governance, trusted execution, feedback loops, and accountable markets.

## Capital stack

Agentropolis recognizes seven public ontology capital types:

| Capital type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Human capital | Human skill, judgment, creativity, labor, and lived context |
| Social capital | Trust networks, reputation, relationships, community, and coordination capacity |
| Built capital | Physical and digital infrastructure, tools, apps, interfaces, and operational systems |
| Natural capital | Energy, materials, land, environmental limits, and ecological dependencies |
| Compute capital | Models, inference capacity, GPUs, local compute, cloud compute, and runtime access |
| Data capital | Structured data, memory, provenance, context, logs, embeddings, and knowledge bases |
| Agent capital | Autonomous or semi-autonomous agents, skills, workflows, roles, policies, and execution capacity |

## Value creation loop

Capital inputs become production and coordination processes. Those processes produce goods, services, knowledge, signals, revenue, and civic value. Markets and governance review the outputs. Feedback loops then renew capital, degrade trust, reveal drift, or identify depletion.

## Growth drivers

Agentropolis growth depends on more than output volume. The ontology tracks whether growth is durable, trusted, and governable.

Primary growth drivers:

- Labor
- Capital
- Technology
- Agent coordination
- Data quality
- Compute access
- Trust infrastructure
- Governance clarity
- Auditability
- Market liquidity
- Feedback quality

## Externalities

AI systems can create output while degrading the ecosystem around them. The economic ontology explicitly models negative feedback and externalities.

Examples:

- Hallucination
- Misinformation
- Trust decay
- Automation overreach
- Prompt drift
- Memory poisoning
- Resource depletion
- Compute waste
- Governance bypass

## Equilibrium and disequilibrium

Agentropolis does not assume stable equilibrium by default. Agent systems are dynamic, recursive, and vulnerable to drift.

The system should track market state, coordination state, trust state, policy state, resource state, feedback state, and drift state.

## Design principle

Growth is not automatically good.

Agentropolis should distinguish between extractive growth, sustainable growth, trusted growth, coordinated growth, governed growth, and civic growth.

The goal is not unchecked acceleration. The goal is intelligent architecture that keeps agents, markets, memory, and governance aligned as the system scales.
